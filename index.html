Introduction as a Writer

How journaling, feedback, and reflection reframed the way I write.

Throughout my life, I never really considered myself to be much of a writer (or a reader, for that matter). I found that STEM-based coursework was always easiest for me, so I assumed engineering was always my best fit. To some extent, I still think this is true. However, learning to develop as a writer has become one of my greatest influences. For a long time, I treated writing as assignments I had to complete and as last-minute papers that, despite putting in good effort, felt like the least enjoyable part of academia. At the same time, I usually felt confident once I finished papers in school, and though I sometimes dreaded the process, hearing positive feedback and being able to finish a paper was always one of the most rewarding feelings.

It was only when I started writing for non-academic reasons that I realized how much I love writing. This past year, I went through an emotional rollercoaster of events in my life, and a friend gave me some of the best advice I have ever received: to start writing down my thoughts. I began journaling, but it often led to lists of random thoughts, and other journals led to quotes that resonated with me. I even got myself a journal with the sole intention of burning it after I finished writing through it all. Learning to write for myself became somewhat life-changing, both in the process of solidifying my thoughts and the process of reading back and internalizing my words. Even more recently, I have found this process helpful when writing for others, especially now that I am away at college. I learned how to write and send a letter to people at the start of the school year, and I have found that handwritten letters go much further than a text ever would.

When I sit down to write, I have always thought that the idea of starting a paper is much worse than actually doing it. I try to follow a style in which I brainstorm, outline, brainstorm more, outline, and finally write from start to finish. In this method, I generally feel that my writing is finished after the first full draft, and I rarely do more than grammatical edits after finishing a paper. However, in this class, I was repeatedly stumped doing this and found that each project required more and more of my critical thinking. I generally consider myself to be somewhat of a perfectionist, which, for me, means I spend less time editing afterwards and more time writing original and cohesive ideas in my first draft, since my first draft is often very close to my final draft. It was difficult to follow my normal process in this class because I found myself constantly contemplating, rethinking, and revising not just my writing, but my actual thoughts on each topic and even aspects of my own life.

Additionally, feedback has easily become my favorite part of writing. Of course, having someone read my work in general is meaningful, but something about receiving comments and having people see my opinions or narrative and reacting to them is the exact reason why I think most people love to write. It not only makes the next time I work on a paper more appealing, but it also feels great when I can help someone think differently or understand an aspect of my writing better just by reading it.

I work by deadlines and will admit that many of these projects were completed within hours of the morning deadline. Still, I have learned that the best writing does not always happen overnight. Revisions and looking back on my ideas have often made many of my arguments completely different and more cohesive as a result. Looking back, each of these projects are easily some of my favorite things I have done this semester. I hope you enjoy reading them as much as I enjoyed making them.

Sustainability Autobiography

Genre: Written Essay | Mode: Narrative

This paper touches on much of my early life and the experiences I developed moving from place to place. I am blessed to have experienced a number of different environments, and they have developed a lot of my morals in building a more holistic perspective on life and what sustainable thinking looks like in real life. This was also one of my first works where I really learned how to write a narrative about myself while still making a clear point.

Visual Essay

Genre: Visual Web Essay | Mode: Mixed Media

Going into college, I was set on a “work hard, party hard” mindset. It quickly turned into a work hard, work harder mindset, and the project became a way to look at routine, nightlife, and the cycle of burnout in my lifestyle at Notre Dame. This visual essay matters in helped me realize how I can treat myself better, and it showed me more clearly who I really am when I step back and look at my life through what I capture.

Visual Essay Reflection

Genre: Reflection | Mode: Text

While I started my visual essay focusing on the inner vibrance of routine and nightlife within my lifestyle at Notre Dame, my goal was to base it on a short window of time so I could capture what my day-to-day weekdays and weekends truly looked like. More importantly, it was the aspect of reflecting on this perspective of my routine that later helped me realize that building a lifestyle was more about balancing my health and seeing the true side of burnout. That shift in perspective helped me see my life from a more pulled-back, captured view, and looking back on it months later and seeing the changes that had already happened to my lifestyle was eye-opening.

Research Essay

Genre: Academic Research | Mode: Text

AI innovation has been at the forefront of many of my studies in recent years, but for this essay, I wanted to tackle the global side of it by examining why governments cannot regulate AI quickly enough. The legislative process fascinated me, especially after hearing about a peer’s experience interning with a state AI legislative branch and how hard it was to get anything done. The biggest thing this essay shows in my portfolio is that, for the first time, my opinion and thesis completely changed between what I believed before and after researching the topic.

Multimedia Project

Genre: Multimedia | Mode: Video/Audio

Following my research paper, my goal was to expose insight on AI at a more local level by looking at what students at Notre Dame actually use AI for. I wanted it to be engaging while still being accurate and unbiased, and I am proud of getting a diverse set of opinions and capturing them through interviews. This project is significant in my portfolio because it shows how an argument changes when it is told through real people instead of only through a traditional essay.

Audience Impact Statement

Genre: Reflection / Strategy | Mode: Text

Finally, my audience impact statement focuses in on the creative process of making the video and the importance of approaching students in a fully unbiased way. I think the anonymity of the video and the fact that I was a fellow student made others more comfortable to share their true thoughts, was a major asset in gathering the best information possible. Overall, the impact of the video is best suited for both students and professors in learning the true diversity in how students use AI.
